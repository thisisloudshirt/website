Gus hesitated a moment, then broke for it. The crisp air hit his cheeks, numbed his mouth. His surroundings blurred into Rorschach blots of white light, fluorescent orange, asphalt, and steel. She was daunting at first: her gargantuan frame, juxtaposed against compact rubber appendages, suggested that her wheels were mere artifice, a feint to obscure the first vessel capable of fuelless levitation. And her propellers! A pair of mammoth orchids with feathery scimitars for petals. The swoop of her wings, the curve of her underbelly. Gus felt whole. This was it. Then he startled—a single, glinting eye stared back at him from the undercarriage, blinking in synchrony with his own. Some paint had chipped off the landing gear, leaving an empty silver hemisphere mirroring his face.

“I won’t let this go away,” Gus said. The corners of his mouth twitched. He closed his eyes, breathed in hard through his nose, sure the stiff air could preserve this, him, the certainty of it all. That if he existed less, it would exist more. And then, of course, when he opened his eyes, it was gone.

When they found Gus, he was on the ground, his absent hands tracing the pavement.

That’s how he remembers it, anyway. That’s how he likes to tell it, his hands grasping, like he’s back there again. The nurses listen hard, and some of them seem to understand. The one with the slack mouth, set open like an unsettled scar, she seems to think it’s true; she nods and stares with blank, reassuring approval. The truth is—it doesn’t matter, really. The truth is Gus’s.

—JMA
